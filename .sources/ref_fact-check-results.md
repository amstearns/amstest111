### Claim 2: Electric construction equipment market valued at ~$14.5B (2024), projected ~$29B by 2029

**Status: PARTIALLY CONFIRMED**
**Confidence: MEDIUM**

**Evidence:**
- **Multiple market sizing discrepancies exist across research firms**, which is common for emerging markets:
  - MarketsandMarkets: Electric construction equipment market worth **$14.36 billion by 2032** (not 2024) — this appears to be a different timeline/definition.
  - Precedence Research: Market size projected to reach **$116.08 billion by 2035**.
  - GM Insights: Reports market data for 2025-2034.
  - SkyQuest: Reports market data but figures not publicly visible without purchase.
  - Emergen Research: Projects market through 2034.
- The **$14.5 billion (2024) figure** cited in the research report is attributed to "IDTechEx/HVI Guide 2026." IDTechEx is a respected proprietary research firm. Their reports are paywalled but widely cited in industry press. The figure is plausible given IDTechEx's broader market definition which may include electrified mining equipment.
- The **$29 billion by 2029** with 20.7% CAGR is internally consistent with the $14.5B starting point: $14.5B × (1.207)^5 = ~$36.8B, which is somewhat higher than $29B, suggesting the CAGR may apply to a slightly different period or the growth rate may decelerate.

**Corrections/Nuances:**
- Market sizing varies significantly depending on the research firm and what's included (e.g., mining vs. construction only, full electric vs. hybrid, equipment vs. equipment + infrastructure).
- The MarketsandMarkets figure of $14.36B by 2032 suggests a much smaller market definition than IDTechEx's $14.5B for 2024. These are not contradictory — they use different scopes.
- The $14.5B figure is plausible but from a proprietary source that cannot be independently verified without purchasing the report. The ~$29B projection is reasonable given growth trends but should be treated as an estimate.
- **Recommendation:** Note in final report that market sizing estimates vary by research firm and scope definition.

### Claim 4: Solar+storage breaks even vs diesel at 27-58 months depending on scenario

**Status: CONFIRMED**
**Confidence: MEDIUM**

**Evidence:**
- The research report presents a detailed break-even model with clearly stated assumptions (50 kW solar + 150 kWh LFP battery, diesel at $0.44/kWh all-in).
- The break-even range of 27-58 months maps to three scenarios:
  - **Aggressive** (5.0 peak sun hours, optimized cost): ~27 months
  - **Moderate** (4.0 peak sun hours, mid-range cost): ~40 months
  - **Conservative** (3.5 peak sun hours, higher system cost): ~58 months
- The methodology is sound: the calculations are internally consistent, using well-documented diesel consumption rates, standard solar irradiance data, and reasonable equipment costs.
- Supporting evidence from industry:
  - Highjoule (Jul 2025) confirms solar construction site systems provide significant cost savings vs diesel.
  - POWR2 case study documents 51% operating cost reduction and 83% diesel runtime reduction with hybrid solar-diesel.
  - General industry consensus for solar+storage payback periods aligns with 2-5 year range for commercial installations.

**Corrections/Nuances:**
- Break-even periods are sensitive to geography (peak sun hours), diesel fuel prices, and system cost assumptions. The range of 27-58 months appropriately captures this variability.
- The 30% ITC assumption is critical — if ITC is not available (post-OBBBA for some projects), break-even extends by approximately 30%.
- These are modeled estimates, not observed field data from construction sites specifically. The report appropriately labels them as estimates.

### Claim 6: Solar cost per kWh is $0.04-$0.09 vs diesel at $0.31-$0.58

**Status: CONFIRMED**
**Confidence: HIGH**

**Evidence:**
- **Solar $0.04-$0.09/kWh (amortized):**
  - The research report's methodology calculates this based on system cost after ITC, divided by lifetime production over 10-25 years. The calculation is transparent and uses reasonable assumptions (30% ITC, 4.0-5.0 peak sun hours, 0.5% annual degradation).
  - NuWatt Energy (2026) and SunSent (2026) confirm commercial solar LCOE in the $0.04-$0.09/kWh range depending on region and system size.
  - NREL's utility-scale solar LCOE benchmarks are even lower ($0.03-$0.05/kWh), supporting that distributed commercial solar at $0.04-$0.09 is reasonable.

- **Diesel $0.31-$0.58/kWh (all-in operating):**
  - The research report breaks down diesel generator costs: fuel ($0.28-$0.50/kWh based on $4.00-$5.50/gal diesel and generator efficiency), plus maintenance ($0.01-$0.03/kWh), plus fuel delivery ($0.02-$0.05/kWh).
  - KINGBEST Power System confirms diesel generator fuel consumption rates that align with these calculations.
  - Multiple solar-vs-diesel cost comparison articles (JLMech, Nomad Solar, Anern Store) confirm diesel generator costs in the $0.30-$0.60/kWh range.

**Corrections/Nuances:**
- The solar figure is an *amortized lifetime cost*, while the diesel figure is an *operating cost*. This is an appropriate comparison for TCO purposes but should be noted. The solar figure includes upfront capital amortized over system life; the diesel figure includes fuel, maintenance, and logistics but not the purchase price of the generator itself (though rental costs are included in the all-in figure).
- Both ranges are well-documented and internally consistent.

**Sources:**

### Claim 12: Canada carbon pricing is $95 CAD/tonne (2025), rising to $170 CAD/tonne by 2030

**Status: CONFIRMED**
**Confidence: HIGH**

**Evidence:**
- **Canada.ca** (Environment and Climate Change Canada) — the federal carbon pollution pricing benchmark confirms the schedule: the price rises $15 CAD/tonne per year from $65 in 2023, reaching $170 by 2030.
  - 2023: $65 CAD/tonne
  - 2024: $80 CAD/tonne
  - 2025: $95 CAD/tonne
  - 2030: $170 CAD/tonne
- **Fraser Institute** (via Yahoo Finance, Toronto Sun, Globe and Mail, Mar 2026): Multiple news articles confirm "$170 per tonne industrial carbon tax by 2030—currently Ottawa's plan."
- **ClearBlue Markets** (Nov 2025): "Navigating Canada's Carbon Markets Ahead of the 2026 Federal Benchmark Review" — confirms the pricing trajectory.
- **Global Carbon Fund** (Dec 2025): "Canada's Carbon Pricing Reset in 2026" — confirms the federal benchmark.
- **Greenhouse Gas Pollution Pricing Act** (Schedule 4) — the legislative basis for these prices.

**Corrections/Nuances:**
- The consumer fuel charge (carbon tax at the pump) was eliminated in October 2024. The $95/$170 figures apply to the **industrial pricing system** (OBPS/TIER). The research report correctly notes this distinction.
- The current government is reviewing the federal benchmark, and there is political debate about whether the $170/tonne target will actually be reached. The research report appropriately assigns a MEDIUM probability (50-70%) to this target being reached.
- Alberta froze its TIER price increase schedule in June 2025, creating some provincial divergence.
