## 2. Cross-Reference Matrix: Key Claims Verification

### Claim 1: Global green building market $618.58B in 2025, projected $1.37T by 2034

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Claim** | Global green building market valued at $618.58 billion in 2025, projected to reach $1,374.23 billion by 2034, CAGR 9.29% |
| **Sources supporting** | Precedence Research (primary source, Feb 2026); SolarTech Online (secondary, cites Precedence Research); GM Insights confirms similar range |
| **Verification status** | **PARTIALLY CONFIRMED** |
| **Confidence** | **MEDIUM** |
| **Notes** | The $618.58B figure is from Precedence Research's "Green Buildings Market" report. However, Precedence Research's own updated page (2026) shows the market reaching $1,481.57B by 2035 with slightly different base year figures. Market sizing estimates are *projections* with inherent uncertainty. The directional trend (substantial growth at ~9% CAGR) is confirmed across multiple market research firms (Mordor Intelligence, Research and Markets, GM Insights). The specific dollar figure ($618.58B) should be treated as an estimate with ±15-20% uncertainty. Note that different reports from the same firm measure different things ("green buildings market" vs. "green building materials market" — $533.77B in 2025). The research appropriately notes this discrepancy. |

### Claim 7: LEED buildings command 31% higher rents

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Claim** | LEED-certified buildings command 31% higher rents vs. non-certified buildings |
| **Sources supporting** | CBRE "Green Is Good" study (Oct 2022, ~20,000 buildings); Green Building News (Jan 2025, reporting on CBRE data); Bisnow (Oct 2022) |
| **Verification status** | **CONFIRMED with important nuance** |
| **Confidence** | **HIGH** (for the CBRE finding); **MEDIUM** (for the causal interpretation) |
| **Notes** | The 31% figure is from CBRE's 2022 study of ~20,000 office buildings. **Critical nuance**: Bisnow's headline states "LEED-Certified Office Buildings Bring **4%** Rent Premium" — this is the *direct* rent premium attributable to LEED certification after controlling for building quality, location, and age. The 31% is the *gross* difference in rent rates between LEED and non-LEED buildings, which includes confounding factors (LEED buildings tend to be newer, in better locations, and higher quality). The research uses the 31% figure without this context. **Recommendation**: Clarify that the 31% is gross, not net of confounders. The 4% direct premium is the more methodologically sound figure. Both the green-contract-correlation.md and market-analysis.md use the 31% figure — the former also mentions the 4% Platinum premium (correctly attributed to Bisnow/CBRE). |

## 3. Bias Analysis

### 3.1 Over-Reliance on Specific Source Types

**Market Research Firms (Precedence Research especially):**
- Precedence Research is cited **14+ times** across the market analysis, making it the single most-cited source. While Precedence Research is a legitimate market research firm, its market projections (like all market research firms) carry inherent uncertainty of ±15-25% for multi-year forecasts.
- **Risk**: If Precedence Research's methodology has systematic biases (e.g., overly optimistic growth assumptions), many data points in the market analysis would be affected simultaneously.
- **Mitigation**: The research generally cross-references Precedence Research with TBRC, Mordor Intelligence, and MarkNtel. However, market research firms often share the same underlying data and assumptions, so cross-referencing between them provides less independent verification than cross-referencing with government data.

**SolarTech Online:**
- This blog/content site is cited **8+ times** in the market analysis. It is a secondary aggregator, not a primary source. Claims attributed to SolarTech Online should be traced to their original sources (often USGBC, UNEP, etc.).
- **Risk**: Information may lose context or precision as it passes through aggregation layers.
- **Mitigation**: Most SolarTech Online claims are cross-referenced with primary sources elsewhere in the research.

### 3.2 Pro-Industry Bias

**USGBC/GBCI:**
- USGBC is both the certification body for LEED *and* a major source of data about LEED's market impact. The organization has a direct financial and institutional interest in promoting LEED adoption.
- **Specific concern**: USGBC-originated statistics about LEED's financial benefits (rent premiums, energy savings, etc.) should be cross-referenced with independent academic research. The 31% rent premium, for example, is from CBRE (independent) — which is appropriate — but the USGBC reporting of this data may emphasize the most favorable interpretation.

**ENR Rankings:**
- ENR is the industry gold standard for rankings but relies on self-reported revenue data from firms. There is no independent audit of "green revenue" figures.

**Company Self-Reporting (Case Studies):**
- All six case study firms have obvious incentives to present their sustainability achievements favorably. Specific metrics like Suffolk's "$2.6B annual green revenue" and Mortenson's "40 GW wind capacity" are self-reported on company websites without independent audit.
- **Risk**: "Green revenue" may be broadly defined. A firm could classify any project with a single green feature as "green revenue."

### 3.3 Market Research Projection Accuracy

**Systematic optimism risk:**
- Market research firms are known to produce optimistic growth projections, particularly in "hot" sectors like green building and clean energy. The 9.29% CAGR for the green building market and 20-22% CAGR for electric construction equipment are aggressive growth rates.
- **Historical context**: Market research forecasts for clean energy sectors have historically been *conservative* (actual solar adoption exceeded most 2010-era forecasts), but individual company projections have varied widely.
- **Recommendation**: Present market projections as ranges rather than point estimates, and note the inherent uncertainty.

### 3.4 Geographic Bias

**UK Data Extrapolated to North America:**
- The Astutis source (green skills gap) is UK-based. The research extrapolates UK workforce statistics (59% of firms struggling to recruit, 35% of workforce over 50) to North America. While the trend is directionally similar, specific percentages may differ significantly between the two markets.
- **Recommendation**: Flag UK-sourced data explicitly when used in the NA context.

### 3.5 Temporal Currency

**Dated Sources:**
- The Kats study (WBDG) is foundational but old. Its 1.8-2% premium finding may not reflect current conditions (premiums may be even lower now).
- GSA's 2018 impact study is 8 years old; federal building performance may have changed.
- Brasfield & Gorrie's LEED Lessons Learned blog is from April 2020.
- **Recommendation**: Note dates prominently for older sources and indicate whether findings are likely still directionally valid.

### 4.3 Data Points That Should Be Corrected or Clarified

1. **31% LEED rent premium**: The research should note this is the *gross* difference between LEED and non-LEED buildings. The CBRE study found a **4% direct premium** attributable to LEED certification after controlling for building quality, age, and location. Both figures are valid but answer different questions. The market analysis (Section 4.3) states "$2.91/sq ft vs. $2.16/sq ft — a 35% premium" which is the LA-specific gross figure (not 31%). The green-contract-correlation.md correctly presents both the 31% and 4% figures but should lead with the 4% net premium.

2. **"40–55% of mid-size firms" adoption figure**: Should be explicitly labeled as an estimate/synthesis, not a data point. The research already tags this as MEDIUM, but it should be LOW given that no single source provides this figure.

3. **UK workforce data extrapolated to NA**: The "59% of firms struggle to recruit" and "35% of workforce over 50" figures from Astutis should be explicitly flagged as UK data extrapolated to North America.

4. **Green building market sizing discrepancies**: The market analysis correctly notes that different firms measure different things ("green buildings" vs. "green building materials"), but this should be more prominently flagged to prevent confusion. $618.58B (green buildings market) ≠ $533.77B (green building materials market) — these are different market definitions.

5. **Mortenson solar capacity**: The research consistently says "12+ GW" which matches the current company website. However, other Mortenson project pages reference lower figures ("seven gigawatts," "10 gigawatts") — these likely reflect older snapshots. The "12+ GW" is the most current and should be used with a date qualifier.
